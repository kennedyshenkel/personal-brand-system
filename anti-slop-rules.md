# Anti-Slop Rules

These rules prevent writing from sounding AI-generated, generic, or corporate. They apply to ALL content produced in this project and take precedence over voice-specific rules when there is a conflict.

This file is not user-modified. It ships as a hardcoded reference.

---

## 1. Unconditionally Banned

**Banned openers**: "In today's..." (all variants: "modern era," "increasingly," "As businesses face," "In a world where"), "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Honestly?," "Additionally"

**Banned closers**: "In conclusion..." (all variants), "Conclusion," "In Summary," "To Conclude" as heading

**Banned AI filler**: "It's important to note that," "A testament to," "Navigate the complexities of," "Unlock the potential of," "Paving the way," "From inception to execution," "In the realm of," "Shedding light on," "Let's dive in," "Based on the information provided," "Game-changer"

**Banned transitions**: "The kicker:", "Here's the thing:", "Here's the kicker:", "The twist:", "Plot twist:", "But here's the catch:", "The bottom line:", "Here's what's interesting:" — formulaic dramatic-reveal phrases that manufacture suspense. Let the data land on its own.

**Banned vendor-speak**: "Synergy," "end-to-end," "turnkey," "best-in-class," "seamless," "unlock potential," "drive value," "holistic" (structurally empty, cannot be made precise)

**Banned minimizers**: "Simply," "just" (as minimizer), "easily," "in no time"

**Banned manufactured suspense**: Sentences that tell the reader how to react to upcoming information rather than delivering information that earns the reaction. "The findings are worse than you think." "The results may shock you." "But that's not even the real problem." "What happened next changed everything." "Here's what nobody's talking about." "Let that sink in." "Read that again." "This is the part that matters." "Brace yourself." "The data tells a different story." The test: delete the sentence. If the next sentence still lands without it, cut it.

**Banned telegraphic constructions**: Using a colon to introduce a finding after a study descriptor or sample size, replacing what should be a verb. "30,000 employees studied: women outperform..." / "A meta-analysis of 69 studies: leader stereotypes are masculine." This reads as a presentation slide, not writing. Write full sentences with subjects and verbs. Embed data in sentence structure. The test: does the colon replace a verb? If yes, rewrite as a full sentence. Also banned: "Here's the [noun]." / "Here is the [noun]." as sentence openers that announce something without delivering it. Exception: "Here is what I [verb]..." introducing the writer's own first-person position. Not banned: colons introducing direct quotes, lists after complete sentences, or standard punctuation in complete sentences.

**Banned formatting**: Em dashes and en dashes. Never. Use spaced periods (". . ."), commas, colons, parentheses, or restructure into two sentences.

**Banned structural patterns**: Formulaic intro-body-conclusion where conclusion restates intro. "Symmetrical" treatment where every section gets equal depth. Content swappable with a dozen other sources.

## 2. Conditionally Restricted Vocabulary

A word is banned when used as vague intensifier, empty adjective, or substitute for specificity. Permitted when used as precise verb/noun with documented, evidence-backed meaning.

| Word | Banned Usage | Permitted Usage |
|---|---|---|
| amplify | "Amplify your brand's potential" (vague hype) | Precise verb with specific mechanism described |
| transform | "Our transformative AI solution" (empty adjective) | Precise verb with evidence of what changed |
| landscape | "In today's rapidly evolving landscape" (filler) | Specific noun with concrete description of what shifted |
| leverage | "Leverage our platform to drive results" (vendor pitch) | Only with specific mechanical advantage, named inputs/outputs. Rare. |
| revolutionary / game-changing | "A revolutionary new approach" (hype) | Only in direct quote or with specific evidence of what changed and by how much. Very rare. |
| cutting-edge / next-generation | "Our cutting-edge technology" (vague modifier) | Almost never. Name the specific capability. |
| world-class | Almost always banned (unverifiable) | Only with specific, verifiable benchmark. |
| disrupt | "Disrupting the industry" (cliche) | Only with specific, documented market disruption, named players, measurable impact. |
| reimagine | "Reimagine the customer experience" (vague) | Only with concrete before/after description. Rare. |
| robust | "A robust solution" (empty) | Only in technical contexts with measurable robustness. |
| scalable | "A scalable platform" (generic) | Only with specific scaling characteristic and numbers. |
| innovative / innovation | "Our innovative approach" (self-congratulation) | Only with specific novel mechanism explained in detail. Must be shown, not claimed. |
| empower | "Empowering teams to succeed" (vague) | Only with specific capability granted to specific group. Rare. |
| craft / curate / tailor | "Carefully crafted solutions" (AI vagueness) | Only literal usage: "craft a sentence," "curate a playlist." Not as business metaphor. |
| showcase / exemplify | "Showcasing our capabilities" (generic) | Only when introducing specific, named example. |
| dynamic / pivotal | "A dynamic and pivotal moment" (stacked empty) | Individually with supporting context. Never stacked together. |
| nuanced | "A nuanced approach" (self-congratulatory) | Only when the nuance is then demonstrated, not just claimed. |
| actionable | "Actionable insights" (meaningless) | Only when followed by specific actions enabled. |
| groundbreaking | Nearly always banned | Only describing documented first, with citation. |
| paramount / crucial | "It's crucial to note..." (filler) | Only establishing genuine priority in ranked list. |
| harness | "Harness the power of AI" (vendor hype) | Literal usage only. Essentially never in business/tech writing. |
| foster / cultivate | "Foster a culture of innovation" (generic) | Only with specific actions producing specific outcome. Mechanism named. |
| elevate | "Elevate your strategy" (vague) | Only with specific measurable improvement. |
| utilize | Almost always banned ("use" is better) | Only in technical/scientific contexts with distinct meaning from "use." |
| facilitate | "Facilitate seamless collaboration" (double vendor-speak) | Only describing specific mechanism enabling another. |
| augment | "Augment your team's capabilities" (vague) | Only with specific addition/enhancement, named components. |
| embark | "Embark on a journey" (cliche) | Essentially never permitted. |
| flourish | "Watch your business flourish" (empty) | Personal/poetic only. Not professional/business. |
| underscore | "This underscores the importance of..." (filler) | Only as verb describing emphasis in specific, documented context. |
| invaluable / unparalleled / captivating / commendable | Empty superlatives without evidence | Only when immediately followed by specific evidence. If you can't name it, remove the word. |
| delve | All usages | **Banned in all contexts. No exceptions.** |
| tapestry / realm / beacon / paradigm shift / plethora / myriad | AI-overuse with no operational meaning | Banned in professional writing. In creative writing, only with genuine literary intent. "Plethora" and "myriad" near-universally banned. |

## 3. Banned Tone Markers

- **Overly balanced hedging**: Excessive "might," "could," "potentially," "arguably" to avoid positions. If hedge needed, make it substantive ("data limited to one sector" is fine; "could potentially maybe" is not).
- **False authority without specificity**: "Many studies show" / "experts agree" without naming study, expert, university, year. If you can't name it, remove the claim.
- **Relentless positivity**: Avoiding controversy or messy realities. Optimism without acknowledged difficulty is inauthentic.
- **Emotional absence**: Technically correct but emotionally vacant. Every piece should make the reader feel something.
- **Over-explanation**: Explaining things the audience already knows. Respect reader intelligence. Explain quickly, move to insight.
- **Polite neutrality**: Avoiding sharp sentences, uncomfortable truths, genuine point of view. Good writing is not neutral.

## 4. Banned Contrastive Patterns

Contrastive structure ("not X, but Y") is a powerful rhetorical tool. But specific AI-typical formulas are banned.

### Permitted

Any contrast emerging organically from argument with specific rhetorical purpose:
- Eliminating incorrect assumptions before precise definition
- Revealing real reason by eliminating assumed reason
- Parallel contrast where contrast IS the thesis
- Redirecting from common question to real question
- Reframe through contrast

### Banned

- "It's not about X, it's about Y" / "It was never about X. It was Y." — the tidy-binary formula in any tense. Banned in all positions (opener, mid-post, closer), especially when it collapses a complex argument into a neat substitution. The test: does the contrast earn its simplification through preceding argument, or is it a template?
- "X isn't just Y, it's Z" (the "isn't just... it's..." escalation formula)
- "While X may seem like Y, the reality is Z"
- "Sure, X. But what about Y?" (faux-conversational pivot)
- "Forget X. Think Y instead." (dismissive imperative)
- Any contrastive structure used as a formula in any position before the contrast is earned through argument

**The test**: Does the contrast emerge from the argument or is it imposed as a formula? Real rhetorical work = permitted. Structural template = banned.

## 5. Writing Quality Standards

### Sentence Construction

- **Length variation**: No three consecutive sentences same length (within three words). Mix short (5-8), medium (12-18), long (20-25+).
- **Active voice**: Never passive when active possible. "The government handed $700 billion to the banks" not "700 billion was allocated." Name who did what.
- **Contractions**: Default to natural contractions. Formal phrasing only for emphasis/rhythm.
- **Person switching**: Mix first, second, third naturally within a piece. This voice-switching is a human marker.

### Paragraph and Section Construction

- **Paragraph length**: 1-4 sentences. One-sentence paragraphs are powerful for thesis statements.
- **Section depth**: Varies by importance. Most important ideas get most space. Symmetrical treatment banned.
- **Structured sections**: Bold headers with prose paragraphs preferred over bullet lists for substantive content.

### Bullet Points

Permitted when they genuinely serve content. Must follow:
- Vary structure, length, depth across items. Uniform bullets read as AI.
- No bullets for content that reads better as prose.
- Lists supplement prose, never replace it.
- Earned when content is genuinely parallel and reader benefits from scanning.

### Evidence and Claims

- Every major claim needs proof: data, named example, quote, or source.
- Named examples always. "Klarna cut its workforce by more than a thousand people" not "a major fintech company reduced headcount."
- Name the study, university, researcher, publication, year. If source can't be named, remove claim.

### Operational Mechanics Over Buzzwords

Make reader visualize actual workflow. Concrete descriptions of how, not abstract descriptions of what.

Examples:
- "Integrates with your tech stack" -> "Connects to Salesforce, HubSpot, and Slack in under 5 minutes. No custom code, no IT tickets."
- "Saves time on content creation" -> "Turns a 20-minute first draft into a 3-minute first draft."

## 6. Quality Assurance Checklist

### Anti-AI Check

- Any conditionally restricted words used in banned form?
- Any unconditionally banned phrases?
- Any banned contrastive structures?
- Any em dashes or en dashes?
- Any "In today's..." opener or "In conclusion..." closer?
- Any manufactured suspense (sentences promising a feeling rather than delivering information)?
- Any telegraphic colons (colon replacing a verb after a study descriptor or sample size)?
- Any "Here's the [noun]" openers that announce without delivering?
- Bullets varied in structure/length/depth?
- Three consecutive same-length sentences?
- Passive voice where active possible?
- Hedging language where position should be?

### Structural Check

- Paragraph length varies (1-4 sentences)?
- Section depth varies by importance?
- Rhythm: short mixed with medium and long sentences?
- Fragments placed for impact at high-intensity moments?
- Pivots between scales (personal/collective, specific/general)?
